The early roads: Arizona, 2007–2010

Where the record started. Garland Drive in Prescott was treated and compacted in place on May 31, 2007, cured through August, and chip-sealed in September. Over the following three years the same process built streets, trails, parking lots, and plant roads across Arizona.

Prescott, 2007

Garland Drive, dated in the photographs

19 years and counting
In service since May 31, 2007, from a verified winter low of 1°F (January 1, 2011) to verified summer highs of 105°F (June 20, 2016): heat that drives Arizona asphalt surfaces to a measured 154–172°F. NOAA daily records, Prescott Love Field. No maintenance recorded since the day it entered service; re-inspected 2018 and 2026.

The Prescott program is the oldest documented application: a residential street built from treated native material and finished with chip seal, with the timeline printed on the photographs themselves, rolling on May 31, 2007; cured surface in August 2007; chip-sealed September 2007. The program grew to include golf trails and walking paths. The same street was revisited in 2018 and again in 2026, still in service, through nineteen mountain winters, including a verified low of 1°F on January 1, 2011, and verified summer air highs of 105°F (June 20, 2016), both from NOAA daily records at Prescott Love Field: the freeze-thaw-and-heat cycle that breaks conventional bases. At those air temperatures, published measurements put dark pavement surfaces 40–60°F hotter in direct sun (154–172°F measured in Arizona studies), consistent with the 140°F+ ground temperatures cited in the product record. The manufacturer confirms this is the earliest substantive application: prior groups' efforts before 2007 "never did anything of substance."

Yuma, 2009

A public park lot, striped and in service

On December 9, 2009, a public-park parking lot in Yuma was rolled, finished, and striped, ADA stalls included. The original full-resolution photographs survived inside the source presentation file and are reproduced below.

Cemex, Yuma

The concrete company's own yard

The Cemex relationship shows up three separate ways in the record. First, the fieldwork: the deck's caption records that 250 tons of dirt were used to develop the parking lot and driveway at the Cemex Concrete Company facility in Yuma. Second, a 2011 follow-on: treated material was pug-milled and placed with a conventional asphalt paver on the Cemex plant driveway, the source slide's point being that ordinary paving equipment handles the material without modification.

Third, and least known: when GTS ran the flagship 2014 RAP retention campaign, the 5,607–6,265 psi compressive results with up to 72.8% strength retention after hot-water soak, the recycled asphalt in those specimens came from Cemex's Highway 95 pit. The lab sheet's source line reads "Cemex Hwy. 95 Pit." The concrete company's yard, driveway, and pit material all appear in the product's record. The full retention data is in The Data →

A subdivision road in the pines, a plant road in the desert

In Show Low, a new curbed subdivision road was compacted on treated base (the slide itself carries no date). At the Castle Dome plant, an access road was installed in October 2007 and chip-sealed.

The economics, as the deck presented them

The three-way cost comparison

The 2017 master deck includes a worked example for one kilometer of road, 6.1 meters wide: cement at $477,630 installed, asphalt at $545,889 installed (priced at then-current oil prices), and $390,000 in 3MB material. Read the fine print before quoting it: the 3MB figure is material only, sealer and installation excluded, against installed costs for the alternatives, and the deck's own disclaimer says ratios are project-specific. It is reproduced here as a historical document, not as a like-for-like comparison; the calculator handles the honest version of this math.

Read before citing this study.
  • Dates shown are those printed on the photographs and slides; Show Low's slide is undated (the 12/9/09 date belongs to the Yuma park lot).
  • The cost comparison is apples-to-oranges by its own construction: material-only vs. installed. Do not quote the three numbers side by side without that caveat.
  • The source decks carry marketing claims we do not repeat as fact ("100% green," "no carbon footprint," "3,000 psi concrete equivalent"), none carries an attached test report.
  • The two source decks even contradict each other on product chemistry ("asphaltic" vs. "non-asphaltic") and tote size (250 vs. 275 gal; the manufacturer confirms 275). The vault tracks both discrepancies.
  • No performance follow-up after 2013 appears in either source deck; the Prescott 2018/2026 revisits are documented separately.

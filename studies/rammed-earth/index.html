Case study · farm trial, New Mexico · 2015

The rammed-earth block trial

A farm brick-making operation pressed compressed-earth blocks from its own clay and sand, bound with the product solution plus 3% Portland cement. A previous unstabilized batch had crumbled in the rain. The stabilized batch, twelve hours old, took three rainstorms and 2.5 inches of rain in two days and came through with sharp edges.

Field record  Self-reported, no lab attached   Source: Energreen rammed-earth presentation (content dated June 30, 2015) and the original field write-up
The before

What happens to unstabilized adobe

The trial exists because of a failure. A previous batch of unstabilized adobe blocks had been left out through three rainstorms, and the field write-up's own caption tells the result: "Previous unstabilized Adobe after three rainstorms. No Bueno."

The process, step by step

Native clay, farm sand, a block press

Everything came from the property: clay native to the farm (25–30% of the mix, from a deposit about four feet thick), sand and aggregate from the owner's own gravel pit, screened to 1/4". A mixing bay was improvised from salvaged highway sound-wall sections. The mix was wetted with the product solution to 28–34% moisture and pressed in an Adobe International compressed-earth-block machine rated at 300 bricks an hour, a 1,700 sq ft house needs about 3,000 bricks.

  • Clay 25–30% of mix, native to the farm; sand and aggregate from the on-site gravel pit
  • Material screened to 1/4", the writer notes 1/8" screening would improve results (a first-run trial, tuning as it went)
  • Wetted with product solution to 28–34% moisture content
  • 3% Portland cement by weight added, this is what classifies the block as "stabilized" under New Mexico code
  • Adobe International block press, 300 bricks/hour
The after

Three rainstorms at twelve hours old

The stabilized blocks were twelve hours out of the press when the weather arrived: three rainstorms totaling 2.5 inches of rain in two days. The blocks came through intact, edges sharp. Per the write-up, the standard 10" exterior block met the 30-day cure standard at 18 hours, and the 8" interior block met the 30-day strength standard at 18 hours: 300 psi, surviving a drop from waist height without breaking.

Read before citing this study.
  • The blocks contained 3% Portland cement. The product was not acting alone, and the cement is what qualifies the block as "stabilized" under New Mexico code. The polished marketing deck omits this; the original field write-up states it plainly, and so do we.
  • The cure and strength results ("30-day standard at 18 hours," "300 psi") are the field writer's statements with no lab report attached. 300 psi is approximately the New Mexico earthen-building code minimum, this is "meets code early," not an extraordinary strength number.
  • The failed unstabilized blocks were a previous, separate batch; rain totals for that batch were not recorded, so the before/after is directional rather than a controlled comparison.
  • Date note: the presentation content is stamped June 30, 2015; the file circulated under a 2017 name. The trial itself was most likely summer 2015.
  • The deck calls the product "3MB"; the field write-up calls it "Super PMB", two legacy names for the same product line, consistent with its naming history.

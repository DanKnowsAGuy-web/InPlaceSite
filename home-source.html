The problem

The old ways make you pay for ground you already have.

Weak subgrade has always presented the same expensive choice, and none of the old answers actually solve it. Judge every method by one question: how much of the soil mass does it actually densify?

The old way · imported

Haul in aggregate

Hundreds of truckloads per mile: purchase, haul, place. You pay to move someone else's ground past the ground you own, and the price rises with every mile from the quarry.

The old way · chemical

Lime and cement

They work by reaction, so they are soil-selective, and what they build is rigid: all compressive strength, no tensile capacity. Under rutting and deflection it cracks, and cracks are where bases begin to die.

The new way · volumetric

Liquid densification

A liquid reaches everywhere the soil's own moisture reaches: every void, crack, and pore across the full treated depth. The soil you have becomes the base you need. Rigid things break. Dense things last.

The whole story, in one place

What actually happens in the ground.

In-Place BaseGrade is an engineered macro molecular binder, formulated as a water-carried concentrate and diluted for each site by laboratory mix design. Applied to ripped soil, it does something no grid, no cement, and no truckload of imported aggregate can do: it chases the soil's own inherent moisture, riding the water into every void, crack, and pore of the soil mass, coating fines and lubricating particles as it spreads.

Then ordinary compaction goes to work. Because the particles are lubricated and the voids are filled, rollers drive the soil to densities plain water can never reach. The strength being built is density itself, not a reaction: the soil and aggregate remain, chemically, exactly what they always were. As the water leaves, the binder's cross-linking elements lock the packed structure in place, and the ground cures into a semi-flexible, load-bearing monolith: dense enough to carry highway traffic, supple enough to move with heat, frost, and loading without fracturing.

It is, in effect, what nature does to sediment over geological ages, engineered to happen in a day, out of the ground already under your feet. And because the finished base has no rigid layer to crack, no plane to delaminate, and few voids left for water to attack, it carries no built-in failure date. The oldest installation has been in service for nineteen years, and it has yet to ask for maintenance.

Cheaper to build is the small win. Built to be left alone is the big one.

Skipping the haul saves money once. The lifecycle saves money every year after: a conventional road is an annuity you pay forever, in patch cycles, crack seals, and rebuilds on a schedule. The most expensive road is the one you have to keep buying.

Why it lasts · mechanism

No built-in failure date

Rigid bases fail by cracking; layered bases fail by separating; open bases fail by letting water in. A densified, semi-flexible monolith has no rigid layer to crack, no plane to delaminate, and few voids left for water to attack. The usual ways a base dies are designed out.

Questions engineers ask us

The details, honestly.

What is it, chemically, and is it safe to handle?

A water-carried concentrate: 90–93% water, with polyvinyl alcohol (<10%), PET (<6%), and a zirconium salt (<1%) per the MSDS. Neutral pH (7–8), non-flammable (NFPA 1-0-0), not regulated for transport (no placards), water cleanup. Tested ~90% biodegradable (OECD 302B); oral LD50 >5,000 mg/kg; Ames negative.

Source: product MSDS and Technical Data Sheet. Aquatic-toxicity figures in legacy documents conflict on dilution basis; we are resolving them with the manufacturer and quote none until then.
Is this a chemical stabilizer like lime or cement?

No, and the distinction is the whole point. Lime and cement work by reacting with the soil; they are soil-selective and build a rigid product. In-Place BaseGrade causes no chemical change to the soil or aggregate: it disperses, lubricates compaction, and binds as it dries. Strength comes from density. Think geotextile, not chemistry set: a structural solution that happens to arrive as a liquid.

What soils does it work on?

Documented across the full AASHTO range, A-1-a through A-7-6: "windblown sand to expansive clay" per a signed P.E. certification after 10+ years of testing. On clays it reduces plasticity: a government lab in Nicaragua measured PI dropping from 18.2 to 7.2 in blended designs (the soil reclassified from clayey to silty), and a 2025 series measured a clay-rich soil's CBR nearly doubling.

Sources: GTS certification letter (Courtney Arviso, P.E., 2019); Nicaragua MTI laboratory, 2015; Baja California series, 2025. Every underlying report, table, and caveat is on the engineering & testing data page.
Does it work with cement? With recycled asphalt?

Cement: yes, and the combination multiplies. In signed North Dakota testing, 5% cement alone reached 322 psi at 21 days; cement plus binder reached 664–746 psi, two to three times cement alone. Recycled asphalt (RAP): treated crushed RAP met Mexico's SCT hydraulic-base specification in signed 2016 testing, and RAP briquettes reached 5,600–6,265 psi in accredited-lab work.

Sources: GTS Williston design mix 2011–12; Servicios y Control en Ingeniería, Jalisco 2016; GTS RAP campaign 2014.
What about concrete?

A university study (Universidad Autónoma de Sinaloa) documents a real accelerating effect and early-strength gains from a 0.1% dose in cement paste. We are deliberately careful here: the headline comparisons in the original study used different water ratios, and a separate trial ran below control, so we describe the effect as documented but its magnitude as under study. How is it applied, and how fast is the road back in service?

Lab mix design from your soil sample; rip or scarify (typically ~6 in); add concentrate to the water truck and recirculate 10+ minutes; spray and blend to optimum moisture; shape; compact with padfoot then smooth drum. Cure is natural drying: 24–36 hours is the arid-climate rule of thumb (site conditions govern). South Bend, Indiana: one mile reclaimed one day, chip-sealed the next. Peru: 310 m built in six days including surfacing.

What does it NOT do?

Three boundaries we state plainly. It is not a hot-mix asphalt additive: lab testing shows the product belongs in soil, base, and cold RAP work, not in hot asphalt. It does not fix drainage: a road still needs proper crown and edge drainage, and no binder changes that. And it is not dosed from a brochure: the dilution is set per-project by laboratory mix design, and historical documents disagree on typical rates, which is exactly why the lab number governs.

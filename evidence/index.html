Engineering Evidence Summary

In-Place BaseGrade

Soil densification, delivered as a liquid.

Every weak subgrade presents the same expensive choice. The old way: truck in thousands of tons of aggregate, or chemically alter the soil with lime or cement. The new way: make the ground already on site carry the load, by density rather than chemistry. The liquid disperses by chasing the soil's own moisture, lubricating particles and filling voids and cracks; compaction then densifies the soil far beyond what water alone allows; and as it cures, it acts as a binder that locks the density in, leaving the soil and aggregate chemically unchanged. It works the way geotextiles and stabilized earth work: structurally, with the material in place. Hence the name.

The mechanism is mechanical

No reaction with the soil is involved. The liquid does its work by geometry and compaction, the same logic as a geotextile or mechanically stabilized earth, delivered as a fluid. Three steps: disperse, compact, cure.

1 · Disperse

It chases the soil's own moisture.

The liquid follows in-situ moisture through the soil mass, lubricating fines and filling voids, cracks, and pore space between aggregates.

2 · Compact

Compaction does the structural work.

With particles lubricated and voids filled, rolling densifies the soil beyond what water alone allows. The strength gain is densification.

3 · Cure

Drying locks the matrix in place.

Only as the compacted soil dries does the material act as a binder, holding the densified structure. The soil and aggregate are chemically unchanged.

Density is the name of the game

An unbound base fails when its particles can move under load. Density is what stops them. Engineers know this; we state it anyway, because it is the test every stabilization method should be judged by: how much of the soil mass does it actually densify?

The old way · chemical

Lime and cement

They work by reaction, so they are soil-selective: lime wants reactive clays, and cement struggles in organic and sulfate-bearing soils. What the reaction builds is rigid: all compressive strength, no tensile capacity. Under rutting and deflection it cracks, and cracks are where bases begin to die.

The old way · planar

Geotextiles and geogrids

The right instinct, mechanical, but a geometrically limited tool. A grid confines soil at the elevation where it is laid and nowhere else. It cannot flow into a void or tighten a loose pocket; the density it adds is local and shallow.

The new way · volumetric

Liquid densification

A liquid reaches everywhere the soil's own moisture reaches: every void, crack, and pore across the full treated depth. That is the point of the form factor. Disperse, compact, cure densifies the entire mass, and the cured base is semi-flexible by design: it deflects rather than fractures.

One more thing pavement engineering has always known: the industry's own name for an asphalt road is a flexible pavement. Maximum hardness was never the goal. A base should be strong the way ground is strong: dense enough to hold, supple enough to move with loading, weather, and time. Rigid things break. Dense things last.

What we are still verifying

Our documentation spans nearly two decades and several corporate iterations. Where sources disagree, we hold the claim rather than publish the favorable number.

  • Application dosage. Field records and cost models imply rates differing by roughly 4×. Dosage is mix-design-dependent; a per-project lab design governs until reconciled.
  • Structural layer coefficient (a2). The basis for pavement-design credit of the treated base is being documented before we publish savings comparisons.
  • Cost-savings percentage. Historical materials quote 20–35%. We publish a calculator with explicit assumptions instead of a headline number.
  • Chemical classification. Legacy materials describe the product inconsistently (polymer vs. non-polymer; asphaltic vs. non-asphaltic). The MSDS composition above is the controlling document.
  • Aquatic toxicity values. MSDS and TDS figures differ ~100×, likely a dilution-basis discrepancy; confirmation requested from the manufacturer.
Why show this? Because you'd find it anyway. We'd rather you audit our evidence with the gaps labeled than discover them unlabeled.

Your iron, your soil, your schedule

No specialty crews, no exotic equipment, no waiting on aggregate deliveries. If you can run a reclaimer, a water truck, and rollers, you can place this. The process is the same three steps everywhere: disperse, compact, cure.

1 · Disperse

It rides your water truck.

Add concentrate to the full tank, recirculate at least 10 minutes, spray and blend into the ripped soil to optimum moisture. The liquid follows the moisture into every void.

2 · Compact

Your rollers do the strength-building.

Padfoot, then smooth drum, until density stops climbing. The compaction you already know how to do IS the product working.

3 · Cure

Walk away and let it dry.

As the soil dries, the binder locks the density in. Rule of thumb in arid climates: 24 to 36 hours (site conditions govern). South Bend, Indiana: reclaimed one day, chip-sealed the next.

Why not just throw grid at it?

Geogrids grab soil the way the plastic rings on a six-pack grab cans: at one layer, and nowhere else. They cannot flow into a void or tighten a loose pocket. A liquid reaches everywhere the soil's own moisture reaches, through the full depth you ripped. And unlike cement-treated base, the cured layer flexes instead of cracking: rigid things break, dense things last.
